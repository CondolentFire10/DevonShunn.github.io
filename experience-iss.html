// THE OPPORTUNITY

A Chance to Put an Experiment in Space

The program paired student researchers with scientists working aboard the ISS — not in a passive, observe-from-a-distance way, but as the people responsible for designing and building the ground-based version of the experiment. We owned the hardware, the data collection, and the analysis. Scientists on the station ran an identical setup in microgravity, and we compared what came back.

The question the experiment was built around was fundamental: convection — the way hot air rises and cooler air sinks — is something we take for granted on Earth because gravity makes it inevitable. Remove gravity, and the physics changes completely. Hot air has nowhere to go. There's no natural circulation. A convection sensor that records active, dynamic thermal movement on Earth should record something dramatically different in orbit.

My job was to make sure the ground-based system worked reliably and produced data clean enough to actually compare. That meant building the hardware, writing the data collection code, validating the readings, and then sitting down with both datasets side by side once the ISS data came back.

// THE EXPERIMENT

What We Were Actually Testing

The core question: does convection behave differently without gravity? The answer is yes — but the value of the experiment was in having the data to show exactly how, and by how much.

Convection Without Gravity

On Earth, convection is the engine of atmospheric circulation. Heat a gas or liquid, and the less-dense warm region rises while cooler, denser fluid descends to take its place. This is buoyancy-driven flow, and it depends entirely on gravity to work. In orbit, aboard the ISS, effective gravity is essentially zero — the station and everything inside it are in free fall. There is no "up" for warm air to rise toward.

The experiment built a thermal environment with a controlled heat source and a network of sensors positioned to capture temperature gradients across the space. On Earth, the sensors record the active circulation of heated air — temperature varies significantly depending on height and position relative to the heat source. In microgravity, without buoyancy-driven convection, the air around a heat source behaves fundamentally differently: heat spreads primarily through conduction and radiation rather than bulk fluid movement.

Comparing the two datasets meant comparing not just average temperatures but the dynamic behavior — how quickly gradients form, how stable they are over time, and what the distribution across sensor positions looks like when convection is absent.

// BUILDING THE SYSTEM

Design, Build, Validate, Compare

The work fell into four distinct phases — designing the sensor system, building and validating the hardware, running the data collection, and finally analyzing both datasets together.

PHASE 01 — SYSTEM DESIGN

Figuring Out What to Build and Why

Before any hardware was ordered, the experiment design had to be solid. The sensor placement strategy was the most important decision: where to position temperature sensors to capture the spatial gradient that convection creates, and how to ensure the ground-based setup was genuinely comparable to what could be reproduced on the ISS.

The constraints shaped the design. The ISS system had to be compact, low-power, and reliable over an extended run. That meant the ground-based system had to match those same constraints — both to keep the comparison valid, and because designing within tight limits is better engineering practice anyway.

The key design constraint: both systems had to be functionally identical — same sensor types, same sampling rate, same physical arrangement relative to the heat source. Any difference in the data needed to come from physics, not from hardware variation.

PHASE 02 — HARDWARE BUILD

The Arduino System, Assembled

The sensor system was built around an Arduino microcontroller handling all data collection and logging. Temperature sensors positioned at multiple heights relative to a controlled heat source gave the spatial resolution needed to capture gradient formation. A real-time clock module timestamped every reading so the datasets could be aligned accurately for comparison. All data streamed to a serial logger for post-processing.

Building this taught me something that's stuck: hardware that works on a bench doesn't automatically work as a continuous unattended data logger. The validation phase — running the system for extended periods, checking for drift, confirming sensor consistency — was as important as the initial build. The data is only useful if you can trust it.

PHASE 03 — DATA COLLECTION

Running Both Experiments. Waiting for the Data Back from Orbit.

The ground-based system ran its data collection sessions under controlled conditions — same environmental starting point, same run duration, heat source consistent across trials. Meanwhile, scientists on the ISS ran the matched setup in microgravity. The two datasets were produced independently and then brought together for comparison.

There's a particular kind of patience required for data collection work — you set the system running, you monitor for anything that looks wrong, and you let the physics happen. The interesting part comes later. But getting clean data in the first place is what makes the interesting part possible.

PHASE 04 — ANALYSIS & COMPARISON

Earth vs. Orbit. What the Data Actually Showed.

With both datasets in hand, the comparison was the most scientifically interesting part of the entire project. The ground data showed exactly what physics predicts for a normal gravitational environment: distinct, stable temperature gradients between sensor heights, with the warmest readings consistently at the top and a clear dynamic signature of convective circulation.

The ISS data looked different in ways that were immediately visible. Without buoyancy-driven flow, the temperature distribution across the sensor array was more uniform — heat conducted outward from the source without the active upward transport that convection produces on Earth. The gradient between sensor heights was measurably reduced. The dynamic behavior over time was quieter — less variation, less circulation signature.

The comparison confirmed the hypothesis, but in a way that was more satisfying than just seeing expected results: it showed the precise quantitative difference between convective and conductive heat transfer modes across matched conditions. That's what the experiment was designed to produce, and it did.

// EARTH VS. ORBIT

What Changed Without Gravity

The two environments produced measurably different results across every metric the sensor array captured. Here's what the comparison showed.

// GROUND — EARTH NORMAL

Convection Active

With gravity present, heated air near the heat source became less dense and rose, drawing cooler air downward to replace it. The sensor array recorded a pronounced vertical temperature gradient — sensors at greater height from the heat source registered higher temperatures than sensors at lower positions.

The readings changed dynamically over time, reflecting the ongoing circulation of air through the measurement volume. The system was never fully stable — convective cells continuously moved heat through the space.

// ISS — MICROGRAVITY

Convection Suppressed

In microgravity, there is no buoyancy force to drive bulk air movement. Heat transferred outward from the source primarily through conduction and radiation. The sensor array recorded a significantly more uniform temperature distribution — the sharp vertical gradient seen on Earth was absent.

The temporal behavior was correspondingly quieter. Without convective circulation, temperatures reached a more stable equilibrium state rather than the dynamic, fluctuating pattern seen on Earth. The physics behaved as predicted.

The Finding

The experiment demonstrated the fundamental role gravity plays in thermal convection — not as a theoretical statement, but as a directly measured, dataset-supported comparison between two identical systems in two different physical environments. The temperature gradient between the highest and lowest sensor positions was substantially reduced in the ISS data relative to the ground data. The dynamic variation over time was also reduced, consistent with a shift from convection-dominated to conduction-dominated heat transfer.

For a student research project, that outcome — a clear, quantifiable, physically meaningful result from matched datasets across two environments — was exactly what the experiment was designed to produce. It worked.

// WHAT IT TAUGHT ME

More Than Physics

The science was the point, but the process taught a set of skills that have applied to every project since — hardware, data, and the discipline that rigorous work requires.

01

Building Hardware You Can Trust

A sensor system that works on a bench is not the same as a sensor system that produces reliable data over an extended unattended run. Validating the ground setup — running it long enough to check for drift, confirming sensor consistency, verifying the data log integrity — was the thing that made the comparison meaningful. Garbage in, garbage out applies to physics experiments exactly as much as it applies to software.

Sensor Validation Long-Run Reliability
02

Experiment Design Is the Hard Part

The most important decisions happened before any hardware was built. What are you measuring, and why? Where do the sensors go to capture what you're looking for? What does the control condition need to look like for the comparison to be valid? Getting those questions right determined whether the data would be useful. This is a discipline that applies to everything from physics experiments to engineering tests to any situation where you're trying to learn something from data.

Experimental Design Controls
03

Arduino as a Real Instrument

Using Arduino for data collection in a research context rather than a hobby project taught a different level of rigor. Sampling intervals had to be consistent. Timestamps had to be reliable. The code had to run without hanging for the duration of the experiment. Writing firmware for a data collection instrument — not just a fun blink sketch — is a different discipline, and one I've used in every electronics project since.

04

What It Means for Data to Go to Space

There's something genuinely different about knowing that the experiment you built has a matched version running aboard the International Space Station. It raised the stakes of the ground work in a concrete way — if the ground data is unreliable, the comparison is worthless, and so is the ISS data. That responsibility made the validation work feel important rather than tedious. I think about that a lot when working on any project where someone else depends on what I produce.

// LOOKING BACK

The Experiment That Set a Standard

This was the first time I worked on something where the stakes of doing it correctly were set by someone else — by scientists running a matched experiment on a spacecraft 400 kilometers overhead. That's a different kind of accountability than a personal project, and it taught a different kind of discipline.

The physics confirmed what theory predicts. But more than the results, what I took from the program was a way of approaching any technical project: understand the question you're actually asking, design the system around that question, validate before you collect, and make the data trustworthy before you try to learn from it.

The Arduino skills, the data collection rigor, and the understanding of what good experimental design looks like have all shown up in the projects that came after — from the BD-1 electronics to the sim rig systems to everything that involves sensors, code, and real-world data. It was a good first lesson in how to do it properly.
