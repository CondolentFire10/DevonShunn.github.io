// WHY BD-1

The Most Expressive Droid in Star Wars

Jedi: Fallen Order is my favorite Star Wars game, and BD-1 is a big part of why. There's no dialogue — every bit of character comes from the way the droid moves, chirps, tilts its head, and looks at you with those big lens eyes. Respawn built something genuinely special: a companion that communicates entirely through motion and sound, and somehow makes you care about it more than characters with full scripts.

The moment I finished the game I wanted one on my desk. Not a statue, not a model kit — a BD-1 that behaves like BD-1. Eyes that glow. Sounds that actually come from the game. A head that tilts when you walk past. That meant this couldn't just be a printing project. It had to be the full stack: design, print, assemble, wire, code, paint. Every discipline I'd been building across four years, all in one build.

It's the most ambitious prop I've ever attempted, and it's not done yet. But it's further along than anything else I've built — and the process of getting here has pushed every skill I have to its current limit.

// THE BUILD BREAKDOWN

Five Phases, One Droid

BD-1 isn't one project — it's five overlapping ones. Each phase built on the last, and each one exposed problems in a previous one that had to be revisited. Here's how it actually went.

PHASE 01 — DESIGN

Modeling a Droid That Actually Works

The design phase was the longest and least glamorous part of the entire build. BD-1's proportions in the game are slightly exaggerated — legs that are too long, a head that's too large for a real structural joint to support. Getting from "looks like BD-1" to "can be assembled as a physical object with real hardware inside it" required rebuilding parts multiple times as the electronics plan evolved.

I started in Fusion 360 for all the structural and mechanical parts — legs, hip joints, the body chassis — where tolerances and hardware fitment matter. The head and detail shell panels were sculpted in Blender to capture the organic curves that parametric CAD struggles with. The two workflows had to hand off cleanly, which meant exporting meshes back and forth and keeping track of which file was authoritative for each part.

STRUCTURAL CAD

Body & Leg Chassis

All structural parts modeled in Fusion 360 with explicit tolerances — servo mounting bores, M3 insert sockets, leg pivot hardware, and the internal electronics tray that the Arduino and battery sit on.

Fusion 360 Hardware Tolerances M3 Heat Inserts
ORGANIC SCULPT

Head & Shell Panels

The head dome and decorative body panels sculpted in Blender from game screenshots and fan orthographics. The eye socket cutouts were then taken back into Fusion to add the precise LED housing tolerances.

Blender Sculpt Reference Matching Mesh Export
FULL ASSEMBLY VIEW

Complete Virtual Assembly

Before a single part was printed, the full droid was assembled virtually in Fusion 360 to check joint clearances, wire routing paths, and overall proportions against game reference. Three full redesigns of the hip joint and two rebuilds of the head tilt mechanism happened in this phase before committing to print.

Assembly Model Joint Simulation Wire Routing Clearance Check
PHASE 02 — PRINTING

80+ Parts, Dozens of Iterations

BD-1 is a big droid. Getting the part count down to something manageable while keeping seam lines in places they can be hidden during finishing took a lot of planning. The Bambu X1C's build volume handled most parts in one piece, but the legs still had to be split at the knee — a joint line I turned into a design feature rather than trying to hide it.

White PLA for the main shell, matte black for the mechanical joints and internals, clear PETG for the eye lenses. The AMS handled the color regions that would be impossible to mask and paint neatly. Every structural part that takes load printed at 40% gyroid infill for rigidity without excess weight.

SHELL PARTS

Body & Head Panels

White PLA shell parts printed with 0.15mm layer height for fine surface detail. Variable layer height used on the curved dome to maintain quality on angled surfaces without destroying print time.

0.15mm Layers Variable Height White PLA
MECHANICAL PARTS

Joints & Structural Frame

Black PETG for all load-bearing structural parts — higher layer adhesion than PLA and far more impact resistant. The hip pivot and ankle joints printed at 0.2mm for speed with 40% gyroid infill.

PETG Structural Gyroid Infill Bearing Seats
PHASE 03 — ASSEMBLY

Making It Stand Up and Stay Together

Physical assembly revealed every design assumption that didn't hold up. Parts that fit perfectly in Fusion 360 had real-world tolerances stacked against them. The head tilt servo mechanism needed three printed revisions before the motion felt smooth rather than clunky. The leg joints needed M3 heat inserts and brass threaded rods to handle the weight of the upper body without flexing.

The most satisfying moment in the entire build was when all four leg segments went together, the body clicked into the hip joint, and the droid stood on its own for the first time. It's lighter than it looks — hollow shell panels and an internal frame keep the total weight manageable — but it has real structural rigidity. Nothing flexes that shouldn't.

LEG ASSEMBLY

Four-Segment Leg Structure

Each leg: four printed segments, two pivot joints, brass rod axles, and M3 hardware throughout. The ankle has a subtle flex designed in to give the droid the slight "alive" lean that BD-1 has in the game.

Brass Rod Axles Heat Inserts Pivot Joints
HEAD MECHANISM

Tilt Servo & Neck Joint

A single MG90S servo drives a 30° head tilt through a printed linkage. The neck joint uses a 608 bearing for smooth rotation, with a slip ring feeding power and signal to the head LEDs through the rotating axis so wires don't bind.

MG90S Servo 608 Bearing Slip Ring
PHASE 04 — ELECTRONICS & CODE

Bringing It to Life

The electronics are what make BD-1 feel real rather than look real. Two Neopixel LEDs behind the clear PETG eye lenses cycle through BD-1's signature blue-white eye glow. A DFPlayer Mini module drives a small 2W speaker loaded with audio extracted directly from the game — the chirps, the boot sound, the beeps when he finds a stim canister. A PIR motion sensor triggers reactions when someone walks past. And the head tilt servo runs an idle animation loop that keeps the droid looking subtly alive when nothing else is happening.

Everything runs from an Arduino Nano on a custom perfboard that fits the internal electronics tray. A 2S LiPo with a TP4056 charge board powers the whole system through a hidden panel-mounted USB-C port. Total current draw at full activity sits comfortably under 800mA.

PHASE 05 — PAINT & FINISH

The Part That Takes the Longest

BD-1's paint job in the game has a specific character: bright white as the base, with yellow-gold accent panels, weathering that reads as field wear rather than neglect, and subtle panel shading that makes the curved surfaces look three-dimensional. Matching it on a physical build meant breaking down the paint into precise mask stages and not rushing any of them.

The finishing pipeline: 220 grit sand, filler primer, 400 sand, primer again, 800 wet sand until smooth. Matte white base. Tamiya yellow for the accent panels, masked off. Oil paint wash into the panel lines. Dry brushed silver along edges and high-wear areas. Matte clear coat over everything, then a spot gloss clear on the eye lenses only to make them look wet and alive. This phase is still ongoing — the body and head are painted, the legs are primed and waiting.

// ELECTRONICS SYSTEM

What's Inside

Six discrete electronic systems work together to make BD-1 behave like the game character — each one designed to fit inside the physical constraints of the printed shell.

💡

Eye LEDs

Two Neopixel WS2812B LEDs behind clear PETG lenses. Soft blue-white idle glow, reactive color shifts on sound triggers, and a startup sequence that matches the in-game boot animation.

WS2812B Neopixel
🔊

Sound System

DFPlayer Mini SD card audio module driving a 2W 4Ω speaker hidden inside the body cavity. Audio files are original game rips — chirps, scanning sounds, stim canister beeps, and the BD-1 startup chime.

DFPlayer Mini
⚡

Motion Sensor

HC-SR501 PIR sensor mounted in the chest panel detects movement in a 5-meter cone. Triggers a random selection of chirps and a head-tilt reaction sequence — the closest thing to BD-1 noticing you that I could build with passive sensing.

HC-SR501 PIR
🔄

Head Servo

MG90S metal-gear servo driving a 30° tilt range through a printed cam linkage. Runs an idle animation with randomized timing between tilts — no two idle loops look exactly the same. Reactions override the idle on trigger events.

MG90S Servo
🧠

Arduino Nano

The central controller. Manages all sensor inputs, sound triggers, LED states, and servo commands. Custom state machine handles the idle loop, reaction sequences, and startup procedure — all without blocking execution.

Arduino Nano
🔋

Power System

2S 1000mAh LiPo with a TP4056 charge board and a 5V buck converter for stable logic voltage. USB-C charge port hidden under a removable panel on the lower body. Full activity runtime is approximately 4 hours per charge.

2S LiPo + Buck 5V
// THE CODE

State Machine in C++

The Arduino sketch runs a non-blocking state machine so the head servo, LEDs, and sound can all update simultaneously without any one system stalling another. Here's the core idle loop logic.

bd1_main.ino 
// BD-1 Droid — Core State Machine
// Arduino Nano · Non-blocking idle + reaction loop

enum DroidState {
  STATE_BOOT, STATE_IDLE, STATE_REACT, STATE_SCAN
};

DroidState currentState  = STATE_BOOT;
unsigned long stateTimer  = 0;
unsigned long nextTilt     = 0;
bool          pirTriggered = false;

void loop() {
  checkPIR();         // non-blocking sensor read
  updateLEDs();       // eye glow tick
  updateServo();      // smooth servo step

  switch (currentState) {

    case STATE_BOOT:
      playSound(SOUND_BOOT);
      eyeStartup();
      setState(STATE_IDLE, 2400);
      break;

    case STATE_IDLE:
      // randomised head tilt on a timer
      if (millis() > nextTilt) {
        tiltHead(random(-20, 20));
        nextTilt = millis() + random(2000, 6000);
      }
      if (pirTriggered) setState(STATE_REACT, 0);
      break;

    case STATE_REACT:
      playSound(randomChirp());
      eyePulse(COLOR_REACT);
      tiltHead(25);             // look up at person
      pirTriggered = false;
      setState(STATE_IDLE, 3000);
      break;
  }
}

The full sketch is about 400 lines — the above is the state machine core. All servo movement uses a non-blocking step function with acceleration curves so the head never jerks between positions. Sound, LED, and servo updates each run off their own millis() timers so nothing blocks anything else.

// WHERE IT STANDS

Current Status

BD-1 is the first build I've done that genuinely required everything at once. Every previous project pushed one or two disciplines — modeling, printing, finishing, electronics. This one needed all of them to be good simultaneously, because the whole thing falls apart if any single phase is weak.

The electronics work. The head tilts, the eyes glow, the sounds play, the PIR sensor triggers reactions. The body and head are painted to a level I'm satisfied with. The legs are the remaining bottleneck — primed and waiting for the masking stage that will take the longest and is the hardest to undo if it goes wrong.

When the legs are done, the only thing left is the final assembly and a video of BD-1 reacting to someone walking past. That's the finish line.
