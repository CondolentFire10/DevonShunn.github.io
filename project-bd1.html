// WHY BD-1

The Most Expressive Droid in Star Wars

Jedi: Fallen Order is my favorite Star Wars game, and BD-1 is a massive part of why. There's no spoken dialogue — every bit of his character comes through the way he moves, chirps, tilts his head, and looks at you with those big lens eyes. Respawn built something genuinely special: a companion that communicates entirely through motion and sound, and somehow makes you care about him more than most characters with full scripts.

The moment I finished the game I wanted one on my desk. Not a statue — a BD-1 that behaves like BD-1. Eyes that glow. Sounds from the actual game. A head that tilts when you walk past. That meant this couldn't just be a printing project. It had to be everything: design work, printing, sanding, painting, assembly, wiring, and code. Every skill I've built across four years of printing, all converging on one build.

It's going to take a long time. There are a lot of parts still in the print queue, and everything after printing is its own mountain. But this is the project I've been building toward, and I'm not in a hurry to rush any phase of it.

// On the Files

The models for this build weren't designed from scratch. The BD-1 prop community has produced remarkably detailed files over the years, and I sourced the best available versions as a starting point. What took real time and skill was modifying them to accommodate the electronics I planned to include — something the originals had no provision for at all. That meant hollowing body cavities to fit the Arduino tray and battery pack, routing wire channels through the internal frame, opening a channel through the neck joint for a slip ring, rebuilding the eye sockets to accept LED housings at the correct depth, and adding heat-insert bosses throughout where the originals had none. The files being printed are meaningfully different from anything that was downloaded — but the underlying mesh work belongs to the community that built them, and that's worth being upfront about.

// THE BUILD PLAN

Seven Phases — One at a Time

PHASE 01 — FILE PREP & DESIGN // COMPLETE

Modifying the Files to Fit the Plan

The design phase wasn't about modeling BD-1 from nothing — it was about understanding the sourced files thoroughly enough to rebuild the parts that needed to change. Each component had to be evaluated for printability on the X1C, then modified to house the planned electronics hardware, then checked against every adjacent part it would touch in the final assembly.

The most involved work was the internal architecture. The original files were largely solid — no provision for any of the electronics I had in mind. Converting them to functional shells with routed wire channels, a servo mount, an Arduino tray, and an accessible battery compartment required rebuilding significant interior geometry while keeping every exterior surface exactly as it was.

PHASE 02 — PRINTING // IN PROGRESS

80+ Parts. A Long Print Queue.

Printing is the phase that can't be rushed. BD-1 has a lot of parts — body panels, leg segments, the head dome, detail shells, mechanical joint pieces, internal frame sections — and each one has its own profile, orientation, and support strategy. Some have come out cleanly on the first attempt. Some have needed revisions. A few are still waiting in the queue.

The Bambu X1C handles the reliability side so the only variable left is the files themselves. White PLA for exterior shell panels, black PETG for structural and load-bearing parts, clear PETG for the eye lenses. The AMS handles color transitions on parts where painting a boundary cleanly would be extremely difficult.

EXTERIOR SHELL PANELS

Body, Head & Detail Panels

White PLA at 0.15mm layer height. Variable layer height on curved dome sections to maintain surface quality without inflating print time. Ironing enabled on all flat top faces for a smooth pre-paint surface.

0.15mm PLA Variable Layer Height Ironing
STRUCTURAL PARTS

Joints, Frame & Chassis

Black PETG for everything load-bearing — better layer adhesion and impact resistance than PLA for parts that take stress. 40% gyroid infill on the chassis and hip joint. M3 heat insert bores throughout.

PETG Structural Gyroid 40% Heat Insert Bores
PHASE 03 — SAND, PRIME & PAINT // UPCOMING

The Finish That Makes It Real

BD-1's paint job in the game has a very specific character: bright white base, yellow-gold accent panels, field-wear weathering, and panel-line shading that gives the curved surfaces real depth. Matching it on a physical build means precise masking stages and not rushing any of them.

Planned pipeline: 220 grit sand → filler primer → 400 sand → primer again → 800 wet sand until smooth. Matte white base. Tamiya yellow for masked accent panels. Oil wash into panel lines. Dry-brushed silver on edge wear. Matte clear coat overall, then spot gloss on the eye lenses only to make them look wet and alive.

PHASE 04 — MECHANICAL ASSEMBLY // UPCOMING

Putting the Structure Together

Physical assembly is where CAD assumptions meet reality. Planned hardware: M3 heat inserts throughout all structural joints, brass rod axles in the leg pivots, a 608 bearing in the neck for smooth head rotation, and an MG90S servo driving the tilt through a cam linkage. A slip ring through the neck axis feeds power and signal to the head LEDs without binding the wires on rotation.

PHASE 05 — ELECTRONICS // UPCOMING

The Systems That Make It Behave

Every component is chosen and every cavity is sized for it — nothing is wired yet. Arduino Nano on a custom perfboard, WS2812B Neopixels for the eyes, DFPlayer Mini into a 2W speaker loaded with audio from the game, HC-SR501 PIR for motion detection, MG90S head servo, and a 2S LiPo with a 5V buck converter charged through the flush USB-C panel.

PHASE 06 — CODE // UPCOMING

The Brain That Runs the Droid

The Arduino sketch is already drafted and ready to deploy. A non-blocking state machine manages the idle animation loop, PIR-triggered reactions, sound playback, LED states, and servo movement simultaneously — nothing blocks anything else. Head tilt timing is randomized so no two idle loops look the same.

PHASE 07 — FINAL ASSEMBLY // UPCOMING

The Finish Line

Everything closes up. Panels clip on. The droid powers up under its own battery for the first time with every system running together. The goal is a video of BD-1 standing on a desk, eyes glowing, head tilting, chirping from the actual game when someone walks past. That's what all of this is for.

// CODE — DRAFTED & READY

State Machine, Already Written

The Arduino sketch is drafted and ready to deploy — it just hasn't run on real hardware yet. Here's the core state machine that will drive everything once wiring begins.

bd1_main.ino // drafted — not yet deployed 
// BD-1 Droid — Core State Machine (draft)
// Arduino Nano · Non-blocking idle + reaction loop

enum DroidState {
  STATE_BOOT, STATE_IDLE, STATE_REACT
};

DroidState    currentState = STATE_BOOT;
unsigned long nextTilt     = 0;
bool          pirTriggered = false;

void loop() {
  checkPIR();      // non-blocking sensor read
  updateLEDs();    // eye glow tick
  updateServo();   // smooth step toward target angle

  switch (currentState) {

    case STATE_BOOT:
      playSound(SOUND_BOOT);
      eyeStartup();
      setState(STATE_IDLE, 2400);
      break;

    case STATE_IDLE:
      // randomised head tilt — no two loops identical
      if (millis() > nextTilt) {
        tiltHead(random(-20, 20));
        nextTilt = millis() + random(2000, 6000);
      }
      if (pirTriggered) setState(STATE_REACT, 0);
      break;

    case STATE_REACT:
      playSound(randomChirp());
      eyePulse(COLOR_REACT);
      tiltHead(25);          // look up toward person
      pirTriggered = false;
      setState(STATE_IDLE, 3000);
      break;
  }
}

The full sketch is around 400 lines. All servo motion uses a non-blocking step function with acceleration curves so the head never jerks between positions. Sound, LED, and servo updates each run off their own millis() timers so nothing ever blocks anything else.

// WHERE IT STANDS

Phase 2 of 7

The design work is locked. The modifications are done. The printer is running. That's the honest current state — it's early, and there's a lot of road ahead.

Every phase after printing requires patience that rushing will only waste. Sanding and priming alone will take longer than the printing did. Paint masking on compound curves is its own discipline that can't be undone if it goes wrong. Assembly is going to find the things the CAD didn't catch. And the electronics and code, as prepared as they are on paper, won't feel real until they're running inside the actual droid.

The finish line is BD-1 standing on the desk, eyes glowing, reacting when someone walks past, chirping from the game. Everything between here and there is just the work.
