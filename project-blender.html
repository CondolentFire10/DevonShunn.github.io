// OVERVIEW

From Zero to Cyberpunk Cityscape

I came into Blender with zero background in 3D software. No prior modeling experience, no animation knowledge — just a curiosity about how 3D worlds get built and a specific vision I wanted to chase.

That vision was simple but ambitious: a DeLorean DMC-12 sitting in a rain-drenched cyberpunk street, neon signs bleeding pink and cyan reflections across its stainless steel panels, with cinematic camera moves that made it feel like a scene from a real film.

The project became the vehicle for learning every major discipline Blender has to offer — hard-surface modeling, procedural materials, particle systems for rain, volumetric fog, HDRI lighting, and keyframe animation. Each problem the scene presented became a new skill to learn.

// LEARNING JOURNEY

Teaching Myself Blender

The learning process wasn't linear. I started with structured tutorials, hit walls, dug into forums and documentation, and gradually built up a mental model of how Blender's toolset fits together. Here's how it unfolded.

PHASE 01 — The Basics

Learning the Viewport & Interface

Blender's interface is famously dense. The first weeks were spent just understanding how the software is structured — the viewport, edit mode vs. object mode, the outliner, and the properties panel. The notorious "donut tutorial" by Blender Guru gave me a grounding in mesh editing, modifiers, and the basics of shading. Slow going, but essential foundation work.

PHASE 02 — Hard Surface Modeling

Building Complex Geometry

Modeling the DeLorean's body required understanding hard-surface techniques — loop cuts, edge creasing, subdivision surface modifiers, and boolean operations. I studied reference photos of the actual DMC-12 extensively to get the proportions right. The gull-wing doors, the louvered rear window, and the brushed stainless steel panels all presented unique modeling challenges that pushed my geometry skills forward fast.

PHASE 03 — Materials & Shading

Procedural Materials with the Node Editor

This is where Blender became truly powerful. The shader node editor lets you build physically-based materials from scratch using procedural noise, math nodes, and texture inputs. Getting the stainless steel's anisotropic sheen right took dozens of iterations — mixing roughness maps, adding micro-bump detail, and dialing in the metallic workflow. The neon signs and wet concrete were built entirely procedurally, no external textures.

PHASE 04 — Lighting & Atmosphere

Cyberpunk Atmosphere Through Light

The entire mood of the scene lives in the lighting. I used multiple area lights with pink, cyan, and deep purple color temperatures to simulate neon sign glow, then added a volumetric fog scatter to make the light shafts visible. A rain particle system with a glass-like material creates the reflective wet-ground look. Getting light to bounce off the DeLorean's panels and pool on the wet asphalt in a believable way required learning Cycles path tracing behavior deeply.

PHASE 05 — Animation & Camera

Bringing the Scene to Life

Animation in Blender uses a keyframe timeline — I set positions, rotations, and property values at specific frames and let Blender interpolate between them. The camera animation was the most cinematic challenge: a slow dolly-in from a wide establishing shot, a low-angle push toward the gull-wing door, and a final pull-back revealing the full cityscape. Each cut was treated like a real film scene, with deliberate pacing and compositional intent.

PHASE 06 — Render & Composite

Final Output and Post-Processing

Cycles render on GPU. Each frame was rendered at 4K with 512 samples, denoised with the Intel Open Image Denoise filter. Post-processing in Blender's compositor added film grain, chromatic aberration, a slight vignette, and color grading to push the neon palette. The final export was assembled as a video sequence with a cinematic aspect ratio crop.

// TECHNICAL BREAKDOWN

How It Was Built

Each element of the scene demanded a different set of Blender's tools. Here's the technical breakdown of the major systems that make the scene work.

01

Hard-Surface Body Modeling

The DeLorean body was modeled from a cube using subdivision surface workflow — blocking out major panel shapes, adding edge loops for creases, and using the Shrinkwrap modifier to conform geometry to a reference mesh. The brushed stainless texture direction was baked into a normal map using Blender's built-in baker.

SubD Modeling Edge Creasing Normal Baking
02

Procedural Steel Shader

The stainless steel material is 100% procedural — no texture images. A Wave texture node drives anisotropic direction to simulate brushed grain. Roughness is varied with a Noise texture at micro scale to break up perfect reflection. A clearcoat layer adds the wet-look gloss over the top.

BSDF Principled Anisotropy Wave Texture
03

Volumetric Fog & Neon Glow

Atmospheric depth comes from a Volume Scatter shader applied to a cube that fills the scene — dense enough to catch light shafts but thin enough to stay atmospheric. Each neon sign emits light with a high-strength Emission shader. A bloom effect in the compositor adds the lens-flare glow on bright surfaces.

Volume Scatter Emission Shader Compositor Bloom
04

Cinematic Camera Animation

The camera rig uses a target constraint locked to the DeLorean's origin, so it always stays framed regardless of position. Three distinct camera moves were keyframed across the timeline — each using Bezier interpolation for smooth ease-in and ease-out. Field of view shifts were animated on cut points to create lens compression effects.

Track-To Constraint Bezier Easing FOV Animation

// TAKEAWAYS

What I Learned

Starting Blender from scratch and shipping a finished cinematic animation is one of the more technically diverse things I've done as a builder. It sits at the intersection of engineering precision — getting geometry, physics, and light to behave correctly — and artistic judgment: knowing when a material is right, when a composition works, when the pacing of a camera move feels cinematic.

The biggest lesson was iterative refinement. Every element of the scene went through ten or twenty versions before it looked right. The DeLorean's steel material alone was rebuilt three times from scratch as I understood anisotropy better. That cycle of build, evaluate, tear down, rebuild is the same discipline that shows up in every other engineering project — it just manifests differently in a 3D scene.

I'm continuing to build Blender skills. Future projects include character rigging, geometry node procedural environments, and potentially integrating Blender renders into other engineering visualizations for project documentation.
